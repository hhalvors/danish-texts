\chapter*{Preface.}
\markboth{Preface}{Preface}

\begin{center}\rule{0.28\linewidth}{0.4pt}\end{center}

Had I not, up to now, let every writing of mine that has come forward
independently in the literature be accompanied by a preface---not exactly
because I have expressly reckoned such a thing among the proper appointments of
a book, but because each time it has felt natural to me---I should this time
have been inclined to dispense with any preface at all, since I had nothing to
say that could be its subject. For the truth is that I would rather have
refrained from expressly declaring that what several of my readers will perhaps
expect to find stated here I mean to leave to them to settle for themselves. I
mean this: in what relation the present writing stands to my book
\textit{Om Erkjendelse og Granskning} [On Cognition and Inquiry], published at
the close of the year 1821. If I am to declare myself on the matter---for how I
intend to use both writings in my lectures on the propaedeutic of philosophy
[Philosophiens Propædeutik] does not belong here to explain---then, so far as
the public is concerned, I would say nothing more than this: I could wish that,
in its relation to the present book, the reader would regard that earlier one
as though it were by another author, one whom I had drawn upon and to whom I had
therefore often referred, while yet having a different object before me than he.
For in that earlier work the object is science [Videnskab] and scientific study
in general, and it is offered as an introduction to academic study at large.
Here, by contrast, I have had philosophy in particular before me, and have
therefore had more to enter into with respect to \emph{this} than had come
forward in that other work. ---Finally, I ask that among the errata [Rettelser],
those marked \textbf{NB} in particular may be heeded.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Copenhagen,} the 28th of October 1843.
\nopagebreak
\begin{flushright}\textbf{Sibbern.}\end{flushright}

\grouphead{Preliminary Remarks.}

\begin{center}\large\S~1.\end{center}
\markboth{\S~1}{\S~1}
\begin{center}What is to be undertaken here, and where we set out from.\end{center}

It is the concept, nature, and essence of philosophy that we shall here seek to
clarify for ourselves. We cannot, then, confine ourselves to the question: What
is meant by philosophy? but have to do with the far more telling question: What
is philosophy? Yet we must of course, straightaway and from the very
first, take account of the word's meaning, attending to what is called
philosophy---and has through the course of history been so called, and has
asserted itself under that name. For although the concept we are to seek to give
is to be developed and determined as reflection on the matter itself demands,
still the question---whether what is thereby set up corresponds to what one
calls philosophy, or whether perhaps, with all the validity it may have in and
for itself, it is something quite other than what one has in view by the word
philosophy---can only be settled by taking into consideration the meaning with
which this name passes and holds good. By the name, the object to be spoken of
is pointed out, and one must from the first take care not to end up speaking of
some other object instead of this one. It is most correct, then, to set out from
a given representation [Forestilling] of what philosophy is---a given meaning of
the word.

But the matter itself, thus pointed to by the name and thereby lifted out for
us, we must then surely consider entirely as it wills to be seen, and must
pursue it through its whole consequence, so that the concept, further on, cannot
be bound to, nor in its whole carrying-through be conditioned by, that given
representation.

In this way we do indeed come to consider philosophy in philosophy's own light,
and thus come to lay our own philosophy at the foundation; for we are now to
philosophize our way to the cognition [Erkjendelse] we seek, and in all
philosophizing philosophy at once asserts itself. But this circularity is a
consequence of the whole position of the matter; for philosophy is but one
member within the world-totality [Verdenstotalitet] which philosophy itself---%
according to the concept of it that we must presuppose as given---is to clarify
for us.

{\small\emph{Note~1.} There naturally arises here the question whether the
concept of philosophy ought not to be treated in two quite different places:
first, in the considerations introductory to philosophy, where the philosophical
undertaking cannot of course be dealt with without laying at its foundation a
concept of what philosophy is; ---and second, in the very midst of philosophy's
system, once the speculative fundamental cognition [Grunderkjendelse] has been so
far attained and developed that philosophy---stepping forward as a co-engaging
member in the spiritual life unfolding in consciousness---comes to be considered
from the speculative---more precisely a psycho-biological---standpoint. One
readily sees that, although philosophy would lead us back to the first
fundamental moments and beginning (\textit{initia}) of all things, it
nevertheless itself belongs in the very midst of the world's course, where it
can first arise in the higher life of consciousness, or in the midst of the
sphere of spirituality, and must then show itself as an essential principal
moment in the totality of spiritual life. The question of philosophy's proper
essence [Væsen] belongs, then, there where poetry, morality, and religiosity too
are to constitute themselves for us in their essential belonging-together with
the totality of spiritual life. ---\par}

{\small The question then naturally arises whether we ought not to distinguish,
from this spirit-philosophical and properly speculative consideration of
philosophy's essence, another, preliminary one belonging to a mere introduction
to philosophy---which we might then call the merely propaedeutic, and which would
at the same time be called the merely explicative. This one would merely pursue
the given concept of philosophy through all its consequence and thereafter
determine and expound it, whereas the other would let philosophy itself
constitute itself for us and thereby show itself in its proper essence. This last
would then be the one which, if the very expressive designation the philosophy of
philosophy is to be adopted, would have to be furnished with that name.\par}

{\small Yet to such a merely propaedeutic exposition of the concept of philosophy
we neither can nor will here confine ourselves, nor keep it separated from the
speculative; for it is philosophy itself---not the concept that has formed about
it---that we have before us here, and the clarification of philosophy, if it is
to be complete, cannot rest at that merely propaedeutic level.\par}

{\small In the meantime we must surely seek constantly to remain aware how far
our concept, as it more and more determines and develops itself for us, remains
within the domain [Gebeet] of the merely propaedeutic---so that it can be set up
as valid with respect to the various philosophical parties (Platonists,
Skeptics, Kantians, Lockeans, and so forth), and can thus be laid at the
foundation in the history of philosophy---and how far, on the other hand, it goes
further, and so is already developed according to the dictate of a definite
philosophical fundamental view [Grundanskuelse].\par}

{\small\emph{Note~2.} One will readily see that it would be altogether
inappropriate to give the first and fundamental part of philosophy itself---which
Hegel has called Logic---the name of the philosophy of philosophy, as some have
done. Hegel, too, is very far from having been able to give any occasion for
this, since with him the philosophy of philosophy---though this name is not found
in him---appears at the close of the third and last part of his whole
system.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~3.} Concerning the expression \emph{nature and essence} and its
relation to the expression \emph{concept}, be it noted that what is here most
immediately and first sought is the concept of philosophy and its exact
determination, but that this concept is then to unfold itself for us, or to step
out into the whole sum-total [Indbegreb] of its consequence, or into its whole
content---which is what we have in view by the expression \emph{nature and
essence}. (I refer here to my \textit{Logik}, \S~35.)\par}

\begin{center}\large\S~2.\end{center}
\markboth{\S~2}{\S~2}
\begin{center}A survey of the whole course of what follows.\end{center}

If, then, we ask first of all: What does one understand by philosophy?---whereby
we are only to indicate provisionally just so much that we may know with
certainty what it is that is to be treated, but must also at once come to
indicate the first points to which we have to direct our attention---then we note
that the received concept implies that philosophy is to aim at a certain
fundamental cognition [Grunderkjendelse] serving for a deeper clarification of
the whole of existence [Tilværelse], but that it is to form itself through
thinking and reflection, and thus to become a cognition that is to be aware of
its own inner groundedness and able to give an account of it. In this way, then,
we come to let our consideration take the following course:

First and foremost we take into consideration that 1) philosophy is a whole body
of cognizing [Indbegreb af Erkjenden], and we therefore consider the nature and
essence of cognition in general.

Next we observe that, since philosophy aims at a certain deeper fundamental
cognition that looks toward the last or innermost fundamental relations
[Grundforhold]---yet such a cognition shows itself as philosophy only when it
comes forward as sprung from rational cognition [Fornufterkjendelse], and
especially only when investigation [Efterforskning] or inquiry [Randsagelse] has
thereby been set in motion---we are hereby led to consider the endeavor of
cognition in general in its transition from its first principal stage, the stage
of first, immediate cognizing, to its second principal stage, the mediated but at
the same time higher stage of cognizing called forth by the spirit of inquiry
[Randsagelsesaand]---the stage at which cognition becomes a cognizing according
to insight, and at the same time---since here testing must take place, an account
must be rendered, grounds come under consideration, and groundedness is brought
to light and made good---a cognition in force of grounds [i Kraft af Grunde];
which then lets us see philosophy as that which 2) is a whole body of intelligent
and rational cognizing.

To this attaches, further, the observation that, since this whole body must form
a well-connected totality [Heelhed], philosophy in its carrying-through and
execution must become 3) a scientific cognition---whereby we are brought to
consider science [Videnskab] and scientific character [Videnskabelighed] in
general.

But since---according to the received concept---not all scientific cognizing is
philosophy, then, by asking ourselves what sort of science it is in which
philosophy consists, we come to become aware that the endeavor to press through
to the grounds cannot but lead to the forming of 4) a \emph{science} that in a
certain \emph{all-comprehending manner} aims at ultimate grounds and an ultimate
fundamental cognition, and that it is this above all that one has in view when
one speaks of philosophy; and here, when the question is now raised whether it is
the ultimate grounds and fundamental relations of all cognition, or of the whole
of existence, that are thereby meant, it will appear that---although a twofold
principal task [Hovedopgave] for philosophy has in this respect formed, and had
to form---it is nevertheless in the end only 5) the ultimate grounds and
fundamental relations of \emph{existence}, or as one is wont to say, of \emph{all
things}, in the finding-out and cognition of which the all-comprehending
fundamental cognition is to form, wherein philosophy consists.

This determination of the concept of philosophy, handed down from ancient times,
from which we are thus brought to set out, shows us philosophy as that which
comes forward over against, and in a certain opposition to, the sum-total of all
that cognizing whereby everything is given us and presents itself to us---%
everything whose ultimate grounds and fundamental relations are now to be in
question, and which we are accordingly prompted and called upon to gather for
ourselves into a single overview.

In its pursuit through the various principal respects or principal directions, we
shall next see the concept of philosophy lead to a whole cycle of conceptual
determinations, which can be developed into a whole cycle of definitions.

Thereupon we shall proceed to consider philosophy in its execution. But when we
now first take into consideration what ultimate grounds are, and how matters
stand with our pressing toward them, the question of philosophy's foundation
[Grundlag] and starting-point will prove to have to precede the question of the
execution of philosophy's work---to which, then, once we have thereupon fully
answered for ourselves the question what philosophy is, a consideration of
philosophy's significance in the totality of existence will naturally be able to
succeed.

With this, the course we shall take is fully indicated.

{\small\emph{Note.} It must be noted here at once---what will be more closely
treated further on in its place---that alongside philosophy proper, of which alone
there is question here, there is a whole body of sciences which, without being
philosophy proper, are nevertheless also called philosophical, but which I, to
distinguish them from the former (see further on, \S~11), give the name of
explicative philosophy, and to which I here pay no regard whatever.\par}

\grouphead{Introductory Considerations.\\[2pt]\normalsize On Cognition in General.}

\begin{center}\large\S~3.\end{center}
\markboth{\S~3}{\S~3}
\begin{center}Philosophy is a) cognition. ---The fundamental moments of all cognition.\end{center}

All cognizing aims at an \emph{is}, whereby something, in asserting itself in us,
is posited by us as an objective [det Objective] to which we must ascribe
validity, and which, insofar as it comes forward for us in this its validity, we
call the true---which is then to be taken entirely as it gives itself to us, that
is: shows itself to be.

Here, then, we see an opposition [Modsætning] between a self-conscious
subjectivity and an objectivity subsisting independently of it---one which,
moreover, comes forward for us not only from without but also from within. But
cognition sets in only when this opposition is so overcome that what is comes
forward for the subjectivity too exactly as it is, so that it comes to be for
us---that is, is posited by us to be---just as it is in itself; whereby we come,
in the very act of living in our own individual subsistence, at the same time to
live in the fullness of objectivity, indeed of the true.

The mediating and unifying element between the true and the subjectivity---which
latter shows itself at the same time as an individuality---is, on the side of the
first, the determining necessity with which it asserts itself, and on the side of
the subjectivity it is \emph{partly} the latter's becoming-determined and the
coming-to-consciousness that thereby sets in, which may be called a receiving
(\textit{scimus, qvia accepimus}), \emph{partly} the individual life's own doing
that answers to that becoming-determined. ---But this last is again partly one
that aims merely at a directing of the mind [Sindshenvendelse], partly one that
aims at inner subjective re-presentation and objectification. In the first we see
a subjective reaction and response, called forth by the awakening power of the
determining influence, that aims merely to make the reception deeper and
fuller---both through an accompanying devotion and through a supervening
recollection---as a care that the true may, in all its points and relations, come
to its rightful full power in us, so that in each and all we may be determined
wholly in accordance with the true's own bearing. In the subjective
re-presentation and objectification, by contrast, we see a productivity springing
from the individual life itself, which has its inner ground and mode of activity
within itself, and now, as a producing doing, brings forth and constitutes within
the subjective self-consciousness the inner presentation, in and through which the
true comes to be taken up into the subjective life. (And here, properly, it is
first said: \textit{scimus, qvia facimus}. ---For the rest I refer here to my work
\textit{Om Erkjend.\ og Granskn.}, \S\S~1--3, as well as to my \textit{Psychologie}.)
But what makes it that in our own productivity we nonetheless have, not something
merely subjective, but---by means of and through this merely subjective---the true
itself, is the power with which the true so determines and governs the subjective
life that creates and forms the representation, that it thereby at the same time
presents itself as the valid for the self-conscious beholding.

{\small\emph{Note~1.} When we here speak of an ``is,'' we see here not only an
opposition between an ``is'' and an ``is-not,'' but also an opposition between an
``is'' and a ``shows itself'' or ``seems.'' Hereupon one may at once ask whether
this ``seems'' too must not be said to be, and conversely, whether an ``is'' can
ever be anything other than a ``shows itself.'' Here it would be a matter of
asking what that ``is'' really means; and when we then observe that we can in
general have to do only with a being insofar as it asserts itself, but that now
every seeming too asserts itself, then the question is whether there is here a
differing self-assertion, and on what such difference rests---whereby a difference
between a merely momentary and subjective and a more constant and objective
self-assertion may perhaps be assumed, and within the latter there may again be
distinguished a) a merely sporadic, b) a merely microcosmic, c) a macrocosmic
validity, and only the last is to be acknowledged as the properly true one in the
last instance. But here the consideration would arise that all apparent, all
sporadic, all microcosmic self-assertion must in the end be grounded in the
macrocosmic order of things, so that here it is to be noted that all that other
self-assertion is seen in its true light---that is, in its proper being---only by
being seen in its emergence from, and reducibility to, the macrocosmic order of
things. ---But all the considerations that might thus be called forth by
reflection on that ``is'' do not belong here, but to the fundamental
considerations of speculative philosophy itself. ---This much is certain: that
throughout all our striving for cognition, the opposition between an ``is'' and a
``seems''---an ``in itself'' and a ``not in itself''---and along with it the
opposition between an objective and a subjective, so asserts itself that in the
whole course of our cognizing, as in the whole of the rest of our life bound up
with it, we cannot withdraw ourselves from it. And to this we hold ourselves
here, where we have merely to expound the nature of cognition as it gives itself
to us, and where we take without further ado the existence of a cognizing, with
all the moments therein, as given---just as we take the existence of a philosophy
as given---without entering here into the question of the proper reality of all
cognition and all philosophy, which it belongs to speculative philosophy itself
to decide. ---The concept of cognition we have here given has plainly---this is
still to be noted---validity even for one who would deny all our cognizing any
true reality; for such a one too must nonetheless have a concept of cognition that
he lays at the foundation, and it can only be the one given here; and one who
would ascribe to all our cognizing only a certain subjective validity must still
admit that cognition rests on the agreement of our representing with something
that takes on the character of an objective.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~2.} As an ``is'' forms itself, intelligence at once intervenes,
and a so-called category---or better, an a priori fundamental determination---%
asserts itself. Herewith cognition too at once comes forward as that which issues
in a judging, whereby a something (as subject) comes forward as that which is
something definite (the fundamental determination of whatness), and this
what-it-is comes forward as an other, which is nonetheless, by the judging itself,
made one with the former (as predicate). This fundamental nature of judging is
expressed in the so-called principle of identity.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~3.} The question arising here---how that productivity, having
its inner ground and mode of activity, and hence also the regulative principle for
it, within itself, nonetheless comes to present a real objective---is of
speculative nature, and finds for us its answer in the Idea of a
\textit{harmonia originaria} between individuality and totality. The determining
influence of the objective is indeed only an eliciting one, since it is the
individual life itself that, by its own activity within the individuality's circle
of consciousness, brings about that whereby the objective is to become the
cognized. But just as the whole individuality first comes forth at all through the
total course of things, and becomes, in its whole existence, as that course brings
it about, so too its whole organization---both in inner, psychic, and in outer
respects---is constituted in such a way, answering to the totality, that every
influence coming from the totality, whether it come from without or from the inner
universal, calls forth its own determinate corresponding activity in the
individual organization, or in the microcosm, and this so that it now, according
to the latter's own fundamental nature, leads precisely to such a certain
determinate production that just that determinate objective is thereby represented
by us. So that, although we constantly behold objects in our own soul, we
nonetheless behold the actual objects as they are in themselves, because our inner
representations become images of them, or counter-images to them, inasmuch as our
inner life of consciousness---according to that inner harmony of its with the
totality, when it (be it noted) is in its normal course---must each time bring
forth, out of its own inner working, precisely that nerve-action, with
corresponding representation-action, which produces just that representation which
answers to the object. The individual organism, which contains within itself the
real possibility of all possible representation-productions, is as it were
calculated to work, at every point---that is, in every respect---in such accord or
concordance with every influence coming from the side of the total, that the
former each time produces precisely what the latter aims to call forth (as when an
instrument, according to its own entire construction, renders---though in its own
individual way---precisely the tone that is struck on another). We feel ourselves,
however, in this not as related to mere counter-images, but as related to actual
objects, because we here constantly feel our organism subjected to an affection
which, coming from something other than ourselves, is what calls forth and compels
that action and production of our own soul---so that the affection of our
individual organism, or microcosm, distinguishes for us sensation from mere
representation.\par}

\begin{center}\large\S~4.\end{center}
\markboth{\S~4}{\S~4}
\begin{center}Philosophy is b) intelligent and rational cognizing. ---The fundamental
moments of cognition in their appearance as such. ---Cognition as intelligent.\end{center}

The individual life of consciousness, determined by the power of the objective,
shows itself, insofar as it comes forward over against the objective, as that
which is in itself free, carrying with it an inexhaustible possibility of acting
in other ways than the way which the determining necessity each time brings with
it. The necessity thus refers here to a freedom, and has such a freedom for its
object, since it is precisely the in-itself-free (the in-itself-free imagination
and reflection) that is bound by the necessity; and only thereby does cognition
acquire the character of a cognizing, since only thereby does it show itself as an
ascription of validity [Gyldighedstillæggelse]. [The true, too, comes forward as
the valid and as that which is cognized in its validity only to the same degree as
this free element, coming forward as such, then shows itself to be that which is
determined and bound.]

But in the first natural cognizing, which forms itself of its own accord, and in
which we on the whole comport ourselves as natural beings (cf.\ my
\textit{Psych., ny Udarb.}, \S~26), the opposition between the objective and the
subjectivity, though it may show itself in particular cases, does not come forward
in such a way that it wholly asserts itself as such. Cognition finds itself then,
despite all the mediating element lying within it, at a first stage of immediacy,
and the moments of cognition, though certainly present, are so lost in the
cognizing as a whole that they do not come forward as such. But then neither does
the true come forward for us in \emph{its truth}, nor does it come to assert
itself by its formally acknowledged validity.

In order for the true to attain such a higher governance, the individual life of
consciousness lying on the side of the subjectivity must first come wholly forth
into its freedom. And to this, too, the striving for cognition leads in its
advance, in that we---whether by some other interest, or by mere desire to know
[Videdrivt] and the higher life working itself forth through it---are moved to go
beyond the immediately given, to seek other and more: either merely a) by looking
further about us in the sphere opened to us, whereby not only the directing of the
mind, that is, attention, becomes free, but also, as expectation sets in, the
representation begins to find itself unbound; or else b) by asking after other and
more than what is given in that sphere, because through it we find ourselves
awakened to seek something such as that to which the given points---so that a
spirit of inquiry [Randsagelsesaand] asserts itself, and scrutiny [Granskning] or
investigation [Efterforskning] comes forth, and now not only the representation,
the imagination, but also the pursuit of relations and connections lying within
it, or reflection, comes forward as such. ---But the new that is now in this way
formally sought is sought at the same time as the valid, and as answering to the
investigation in such a way that that questioning can find itself satisfied;
whereby one strives after a result by which the representing and pursuing that has
become free---or has stepped forth into its freedom---can again be sufficiently
determined and bound, with a necessity that is now also sought, and which, when
found, will come to come forward as such.

But the necessity---together with the being-governed by, and being-at-home in, the
power of the objective---that sets in in this way has then a different and freer
character than that first immediate necessity. It now comes forward as necessity
of understanding and reason [Forstands- og Fornuftnødvendighed], and we ourselves
now show ourselves as beings of reason who ascribe validity because we acknowledge
according to an express admission, assent, and approval grounded in insight;
whereby the acknowledgment, or ascription of validity, too comes---stepping forth
into its freedom---to show itself as such. For although at first it may be only new
objects, of the same kind as those given in an immediate way, that are thus sought
under a merely tracking observation, they must now not only show themselves to us
as the ones we sought, but we must now also be assured that they are the right
ones, and that we now objectify them to ourselves rightly, so that nothing else
presents itself to us and is posited by us than the in-itself-valid, or that which
\emph{ought} to be posited. ---But this then naturally has such a retroactive
effect on what was earlier immediately assumed that it too can be drawn into the
same consideration, so that \emph{all} cognizing can become the object of a
question as to its validity.

Cognition will in this way become one that we ourselves confirm in ourselves, in
that we confirm ourselves in it, so that now the assurance too comes to show itself
as such, and indeed as a potentiated assurance, or as an assurance of whose
validity we are assured. ---But certainly the uncertainty lying in the questioning
may here too come forward in such a way that something doubting, dubitative, may
set in, which is however overcome when a result is attained that becomes
sufficiently satisfying.

Here, then, there emerges a properly mediated cognizing---mediated, namely, by
thinking, and resting on thinking and the satisfaction of thinking---an intelligent
cognizing, in the formation of which the fundamental demands originally lying in
the pursuit of connection and relation that strives toward cognition, that is, in
thinking itself (in the intelligence in the subject), are so in motion that it is
in accordance with their satisfaction that insight, approval, and acknowledgment
set in.

\begin{center}\large\S~5.\end{center}
\markboth{\S~5}{\S~5}
\begin{center}Continuation. What the intelligent cognizing aims at.\end{center}

Such an intelligent cognizing forms itself, in all inquiry that reaches its goal,
at least to this extent, that it is seen a) \emph{that} something definite is to
be assumed and ought to be posited. But if we ask whether also that b)
\emph{which} we cognize---the content of cognition---constitutes itself for us
through thinking, that is, through necessity of understanding and reason, we note
that this too is something to which thinking leads. We thus come to distinguish
between 1) the affection-necessity that confirms itself by itself---though through
the medium of thinking or intelligence---together with the cognizing that rests
upon it, and 2) the necessity constituted by the consideration of understanding
and reason \emph{with respect to the assumed content itself}, together with the
cognizing that rests upon this. In the first case the necessity is of that first
immediate kind that preceded inquiry, and it asserts itself in the same way, save
that we have now, through reflection, found ourselves entitled to grant it room
and power. The validity of cognition here comes forward for us on the ground of a
pursuit of relation and connection, and the acknowledgment, or ascription of
validity, has its foundation in such a pursuit; but it does not yet assert itself
in the constitution of cognition with respect to the cognized itself. [As when,
by means of tests, one assures oneself that one has observed a phenomenon
correctly.] In the second case, by contrast, that which is to be cognized is
fixed and determined by the intelligence, so that cognition even takes on the
appearance of being one that we ourselves constitute by our own thinking and
determining---although here too we are determined, since here too that which must
guide and determine this our constituting becomes the foundation. Only the last
kind of cognizing can we properly call the intelligent cognizing, and it is then
one that partly a) takes place in force of the pursuit of connection and relation,
and partly b) consists in a cognition of the connections and relations
themselves, together with what lies at the ground in or for them, which in them
makes up the inner constitutive element.

{\small\emph{Note~1.} It will easily be seen that, while under the first case we
reckon in the reflection that determines us to ascribe validity to a felt
affection-necessity, the reflection on the groundedness of this ascription of
validity---when this is now regarded as the object of our questioning---will, by
contrast, carry us over into the second of the two cases here distinguished.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~2.} We note further that there may be cases here in which a
cognition of a ``what'' can, together with its ``that,'' constitute itself for us
in an intelligent way, without our having penetrated into the object's ``how.''
Thus the validity of our faith in a governance [Styrelse], and the validity of
our living in this faith, can prove themselves to us in an intelligent way---and
this so that the very cognition of the existence of such a governance constitutes
itself for us through thinking, without an insight into that governance itself
being thereby formed.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~3.} Concerning the expression necessity of understanding and
reason, by which the necessity we may also call the intellectual is here
designated, be it noted that the double expression has come in because here one
is to think most immediately of the understanding (\textit{intellectus}), as that
which enters into the pursuit of connection and relation---the both objective and
subjective pursuit of thought that here takes place, and which leads to insight,
and so also to understanding (\textit{intelligere}); but that then, when the
acknowledgment takes place as such, so that there is posited what we see we must,
and indeed \emph{ought} to, posit, the sense for the all-valid in its all-validity
is in motion in the manner of recollection---that is, reason.\par}

But now the objective can also show itself to us through---and address us
through---the beneficently satisfying that it can have for us, and can now assert
itself in us, in its validity, by this way too. We then come to be moved by it in
such a way that we attach ourselves to it, give ourselves over to it, and love it;
whereby, once an acknowledgment of it also develops, a quite different kind of
cognition sets in than cognition in force of insight and acknowledged
groundedness---namely the one I have elsewhere called the sympathetic
[sympathetisk], and have presented as the third principal kind of cognizing, in
the threefold division: a) the immediately beholding, b) the intelligent, c) the
sympathetic cognizing---to which last belong partly the aesthetic, partly faith
and what may be placed together with it. See my \textit{Psych., ny Udarb.}, \S~33,
p.~130; cf.\ \textit{Maanedskr.\ f.\ Liter.}\ 1838, no.~6.

{\small\emph{Note~5.} But this threefold division we must here take into
consideration in yet another respect. We have come to see how cognition, from its
first principal stage---that of immediacy, or the stage of first immediate
cognizing---passes over to assume a new character, by coming forward as the
mediated, but at the same time intelligent and thereby higher, cognizing called
forth by the spirit of inquiry. But now the question must arise how this stands to
that sympathetic cognizing, which likewise shows itself as mediated in such a way
that in it too the subjectivity comes forward as such over against the objective.
We note, then, that this last is here to be reckoned with that first immediate
cognizing; for although, in comparison with the immediately beholding cognizing,
it shows itself to be more mediated---and so far to be set alongside the
intelligent---it nonetheless, just as much as the immediately beholding, forms
itself prior to all inquiry and testing, and yields something that is only by the
intelligent cognizing to be made the object of a question as to its validity and
its content. ---Over against the intelligent cognizing---which, as we shall see
more closely further on, can also be called the rational---we find another, at
which we do not arrive through a rational striving, but which forms itself prior
to it, and has its own foundation, independent of the rational (by which we mean
the rational as it comes forward in its rationality). This other cognizing we may
then call the extrarational or preterrational (because we do not have it by way of
inquiry and rendering of account, or by grounds that have presented themselves to
us as such, but obtain it in another way). To such extrarational cognizing now
belongs both that which owes its existence solely to outer sensation, together
with what is to be placed alongside it (to which the intuition of space and time
belongs), and also the whole sympathetic cognizing, which likewise forms and
maintains itself outside that rationality. All such extrarational cognizing is
also, as regards its arising, by its nature more dependent on immediate influence,
affection, than the intelligent. For the rest, we also find in the sphere of the
intelligent much that forms itself in the manner of immediacy; and it must be
noted generally that humanity, especially among the cultivated nations, now
appropriates in the manner of immediacy, or of itself---that is, without express
reflection and deliberation---much toward which one had at first only slowly
worked one's way. This, especially as regards the music of the ancients, has been
elucidated by the Reverend Pastor Bindesbøl in his review of \emph{Dr.} Bojesen's
doctoral dissertation \textit{de harmonica scientia Græcorum} (1833) in
\textit{Maanedskr.\ f.\ Liter.}\ 1837, 8th number; cf.\ the introduction to
\emph{Dr.} Bojesen's school program 1843, \textit{de tonis s.\ harmoniis
Græcorum}, p.~6.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~6.} The question that here naturally arises, when we look to
human striving as striving and to its course and fate in humanity---why, then, not
all cognizing, including the deeper and higher, gives itself to us in the simpler,
more direct, and more immediate way, as the immediacy of genius nonetheless shows
to happen in individual cases in the higher spheres---has been treated by me in my
work \textit{Om Erkj.\ og Gr.} in the Note to \S~4, pp.~32--36, with which what is
set forth in my \textit{Pathologie}, \S~23, pp.~55--56, may be compared. But I
will here return to this question, in order to give a more comprehensive answer to
it than was there given.\par}

Three things are here to be looked to: We note that we must not only a) work our
way slowly, through much investigation and exertion, toward the higher cognizing,
but that the way even b) passes through much doubt and uncertainty, indeed c)
opens such a possibility of error that this has in many ways become actual; and we
now ask why the whole state of affairs is such. To this the answer is: a) that it
thus belongs to the individual spiritual course of development on the one side,
and to the full objectification of the objective on the other, so that both the
full advance of personality on its side and the advance of the realm of the
objective on its side must bring it about so; and that, as regards b) doubt in
particular, the full self-confirmation and confirmation of the matter can take
place only in this way, point by point; and that c) the possibility of error could
be nothing other than a direct consequence of the whole state of affairs, since
this entailed that reflection and imagination had to become free and to assert
themselves in their freedom, in order that a life in intelligence and freedom
might develop and form itself---but that such a life was in turn the necessary
foundation for the development of a realm in spirit and love.

But finally it remains to be noted that, when one looks to the condition of
humanity as it actually shows itself to be, it must surely be taken into account
that the general human sinfulness, and the abnormality or aberration and
disturbance bound up with it in the whole of human life, could not but intervene
inhibitingly, disturbingly, and distortingly in the human striving for cognition,
and indeed in the whole course and manner of cognition---and that this must be
taken into account when we set actuality before our eyes and find it full of
uncertainty, doubt, and error.

\begin{center}\large\S~6.\end{center}
\markboth{\S~6}{\S~6}
\begin{center}Cognition as rational, working toward an ultimate foundation.\end{center}

Insofar as the questioning and inquiry that set in lead to the testing under which
we render account of the validity of the new becoming-determined that is to set
in, the determining shows itself as a ground, or as that whereby we, in rendering
account, find ourselves determined to assume what we assume (\textit{ratio}); and
insofar as such a determining takes place point by point in the whole of the new
cognizing now forming itself, there set in here, in a continuous way, grounds
without cease (\textit{rationes}), and cognition then shows itself as a cognition
in force of grounds---and indeed as one that asserts itself through the
consciousness, now coming forward as such, of its groundedness. The intelligent
cognizing then shows itself, with respect both to the rendering of account and to
the consideration of grounds that here takes place, as a rational one. The
in-itself grounded and valid is then assumed only insofar as it has shown itself
to us in its groundedness, and thereby in its truth.

But when grounds are sought and pursued, and a full assurance is to be sought in
groundedness, then one must go back to ultimate grounds and an ultimate
foundation---which is then thereafter to be regarded as the first foundation, and
the grounds as the first grounds for the whole cognizing, which only thereby
becomes thoroughly rational.

{\small\emph{Note.} The in-itself-valid true thereby comes---as it now steps
forward in its acknowledged validity and truth---to rule with a higher power, in
that it comes to rule as that to which we ourselves render our homage. ---But the
resisting subjective too is thereby lifted to a higher stage; for since cognition
here proceeds from inquiry and testing, and the agreement and assent that follow
from them, the subjectivity comes to move with freedom and with harmony in that
sphere of higher necessity and being-governed. ---The governed is now ensouled,
inspirited.\par}

\begin{center}\large\S~7.\end{center}
\markboth{\S~7}{\S~7}
\begin{center}Philosophy is c) scientific cognition. On science and scientific
character in general.\end{center}

If we consider more closely what grounds are, we see that---since they consist in
that which brings it about that, when something definite is given, some definite
other must also be, or be posited to be---they point back to a certain connection,
and this again to a certain fundamental connection, according to which it is that
with or from the one the other follows; so that the cognition of grounds must
become a cognition of connection, and cognition in force of grounds must become a
cognizing in force of the pursuit of connection. But thus the rational cognizing
must lead to the whole body of cognitions one has becoming a well-connected one,
in which each particular, as an account is given of it, is at the same time seen
in its being taken up into a forming totality of cognition. Hereby cognition
becomes scientific, and the whole body of cognition becomes science in the proper
sense. ---Hereby a plurality of cognitions---which, insofar as they come forward
each on its own with its determinateness, make up the detail [Detail] of the
science---orders itself together in such a way that a well-connected whole, or a
system, forms itself; in which, however, the cognizing itself, the very insight
and rationality according to which every particular is determined and the whole
system is ordered and formed, is so much the main thing that everything depends on
such a thing---which we now call sometimes the spirit of the science, sometimes
its Idea---coming to stir and live in that totality of cognitions; in which,
however, every particular must now come forth each on its own, so that the detail
of the science, by an intelligent and rational pursuit of the matter's own detail,
unfolds itself into a whole body of concepts and conceptual determinations, and
the system shows itself as a whole of conceptual cognizing answering to the
objectively given whole.

{\small\emph{Note.} To the threefold division here named as lying in science as
such---a) spirit or Idea, b) system, and c) detail---there will further be added,
when one looks to the so-called treatment of a science (both in the study that is
to lead to it and in the communication of it to others), method [Methode], which
may then be regarded partly in a subjective-logical, partly in a didactic respect.
But herewith there is no longer question of the matter itself, but only of our
endeavor of cognition and presentation. Concerning both that objective threefold
division and this subjective element, I refer for the rest to my book
\textit{Om Erkj.\ og Granskn.}, where there is also (in \S~17) discussion of the
inner organic relation of reciprocal action among Idea, system, and detail. Only
on this relation of reciprocal action will I dwell somewhat here, and I note the
following about it:\par}

We find ourselves in the midst of a great totality of objects and relations, and
this not only in the outer respect, but also with respect to the realm of
intelligence and of the higher manifestations and workings within us. Our life
forms itself under these conditions in a sporadic way, from innumerable points
that assert themselves, each point in its own way; and to this there corresponds a
whole body of observations or remarks, each of which then comes forward with its
own---actual, or at least presumed---validity. Such observations can further be
made deliberately at innumerable points, since each time that is observed and
apprehended which each time comes forward. Only when a whole body of such
observations has been gathered can that spirit of pursuit begin to work which is
to lead to the well-connected total cognition, to system and systematic insight.
Here the isolated observation of every particular in its particularity will
constantly assert its right, so that the systematic cognizing must become such as
the detailed observing brings it about; but conversely the systematic insight will
in turn have a determining, clarifying, purifying influence on the detailed
observing. The whole will thus, in and for our cognizing, be determined by the
detail, and the cognition of detail, together with the way of seeing and setting
forth the detail, be in turn determined by the cognition of the whole.

But in the whole, as the gathering-together or coming-together of the manifold
into unity, there must further reveal itself that in force of which both the
particular is taken as it is taken---being in a determinate way taken up into the
whole and ordered within it---and the whole is organized as it is thereupon
carried out. And thus we come to something more deeply underlying: the, as it
were, omnipresent fundamental cognition, which rises up beneath the whole
observing and organizing, and now constitutes both the fundamentally determining
and the fundamentally guiding, and yet at the same time itself only develops and
takes on definite shape in the course of that striving. This we now call sometimes
the Idea, sometimes the spirit in the science.

%%  THE PROPAEDEUTIC CONCEPT OF PHILOSOPHY AND ITS EXPOSITION. (§§ 8--14, pp. 25--58)

\grouphead{The Propaedeutic Concept of Philosophy and Its Exposition.}

\begin{center}\large\S~8.\end{center}
\markboth{\S~8}{\S~8}
\begin{center}Philosophy aims d) at an all-comprehending fundamental cognition.
The subjective principal task.\end{center}

A whole body of cognitions can be intelligent and rational, and moreover so
entirely scientific that it leads to the development of a proper science, without
therefore being philosophy---indeed without so much as coming into contact with
philosophy, according to the prevailing concept of it, to which we here (in
accordance with what was noted in \S~1) have to hold ourselves. Of this we find
telling proofs both in the natural sciences and in mathematics. The question then
is what is peculiar to the science that is called philosophy.

We note first of all that philosophy is to lead us back to the ultimate grounds.
But here the question now arises what is meant by this, and the following is then
to be taken into consideration:

It was cognition that, under the rational striving, first and most immediately
showed itself as that which passed over into becoming one that came to take place
in force of grounds; it was cognition that was to show itself to us in its
groundedness and validity, and thereby in its truth. So far, then, the grounds in
question were grounds of knowing (\textit{rationes cognoscendi}). Here the
constant question is: what are we to assume and posit, and by what right do we
each time assume what we assume and, according to this assuming, cognize; but the
endeavor here presses toward an ultimate foundation.

By this we are now first and most immediately led to look to the first source or
origin of cognitions---by which is meant that whereby they are given us, or which
exercises the determining influence upon us. But here, in different spheres of
cognition, a different kind of immediate determinateness comes forward, which in
each sphere by itself yields something first, from which we, by pursuing the
relations that show themselves therein, are led to more; and this more, when it
now comes forward for us, either a) asserts itself by the same kind of immediate
determining as that first, so that the mediating becomes merely a leading-on, or
else b) shows itself as that which is assumed in an intelligent way on the ground
of that first, or is assumed as something seen to have to be a consequence of it,
even though this does not stand before us in the same immediate way as that first.
In the first case the testing can aim only at whether we really are determined
(affected) as it presents itself to us (whether we really do see, hear, and so on,
this and that so and thus); in the second case the testing aims at that which is
to be assumed in such a way that it must be seen to correspond to and agree with
the grounding referred to it, so that here it is a question of intelligent
satisfaction (for example, in determining the representation one is to form of the
planetary system). See above, \S~5.

But of a quite different nature is the pursuit and the question concerning the
validity of our cognizing, when the dubitative that may here arise, and the
problematic (especially under great collisions, where from one side one thing,
from another another, seems bent on asserting itself), lead to asking after the
validity of all human cognizing---indeed after the groundedness of all assurance
whatsoever---so that the reliability of all those relatively first sources, and
the validity of their authority, indeed the objective truth of everything given
through them, are made the object of a question and a consideration that then
becomes so all-comprehending that an ultimate foundation for all cognizing as
cognizing, or for all assuming and ascription of validity whatsoever, and an
all-comprehending fundamental cognition in this respect, are demanded. But one
easily sees that then there is no longer question of grounds of knowing, but of
all cognition's own groundedness and validity, so that here there is even question
of something other and more than what we may call the \textit{universam
cognoscendi rationem}, when by this, namely, we mean the fundamental nature and
constitution of all cognizing. Hereby we pass over into the domain of philosophy;
indeed both the nature of the matter itself and a glance at the whole history of
philosophy must lead us to set up the question here arising as one of philosophy's
principal tasks, which we will then call philosophy's subjective principal task.
But of this we shall speak further on, where it will appear that this task is
taken up into a larger one.

\begin{center}\large\S~9.\end{center}
\markboth{\S~9}{\S~9}
\begin{center}The objective principal task. ---Philosophy aims e) at the ultimate
grounds of existence.\end{center}

For the whole rationality in our cognizing aims at more than merely the
groundedness and validity of cognitions. It aims at the fundamental relations of
the objects themselves, and thus also at these objects' own grounds, in that we
strive that what each point in existence itself is grounded in may show itself to
us. For our whole endeavor of cognition aims at our being able, in and through
cognition, fully to appropriate and to live our way into objectivity; but then we
must enter into the connection and relations in all that is given, so that
everything therein may show itself to us, each in its significance within the
totality. And thus we come not only to call ourselves to account with respect to
our apprehension of the objective, but come also to call the objective itself to
account at every point---to call the things themselves to account---that they may,
as it were, give us a reckoning of what they themselves are grounded in, and how
it stands with their fundamental relations. ---But since we now also here strive to
go to ultimate grounds and fundamental relations, we come---since the whole of
existence asserts itself to us as forming a single whole---to strive after an
all-comprehending fundamental cognition corresponding to it: not as an aggregate
of several, but with inner unity and wholeness in itself.

And hereby we again pass into the domain of philosophy and meet its second
principal task, which we will call the objective.

\begin{center}\large\S~10.\end{center}
\markboth{\S~10}{\S~10}
\begin{center}A propaedeutic definition of philosophy.\end{center}

Of philosophy in general we may then say that it arises when the endeavor a) to go
to the ultimate grounds also aims b) at becoming all-comprehending, and this in
such a way that it would lead everything back to c) one universal foundation, so
that one all-comprehending ultimate fundamental cognition may form itself. And
herewith the first propaedeutic definition of philosophy is given.

But when we now add that philosophy, as we have seen, and as the history of
philosophy too teaches, has from the first a twofold principal task---since both
the question of the ultimate foundation and groundedness, reality and objectivity,
truth and certainty of all our cognizing, and the question of the ultimate grounds
and fundamental relations of the things themselves, come forward as chief and
fundamental questions---we come, by considering the relation of the two principal
tasks to one another, to see that the subjective task is to be subsumed under the
objective. For the ultimate ground and constitution of all our cognizing
(\textit{universa ratio cognoscendi}) must after all rest on our whole standing
with respect to the objective, our whole incorporation into and fundamental
relation to the totality that presents itself to us; so that, just as we find
ourselves only as members and parts of a great whole, so too we must find our
consciousness, and thus also our cognizing, so entering into this whole that our
whole life of consciousness, with all our cognition in its wholeness, can become
only one among the chief objects whose ultimate fundamental relations and
fundamental essence are to be treated.

Thus our definition will determine itself more closely as follows: that we regard
philosophy in general as that whose task it is, in an all-comprehending way, to
press through to the ultimate grounds and fundamental relations of the whole of
existence, or of all things, in order, in force of the fundamental cognition
thereby won, to clarify everything. ---Philosophizing is then the thinking
consideration, or pursuit of thought, in which partly a striving to reach such an
ultimate---or, as it may in another respect also be called, first---fundamental
cognition works itself forward, and partly such a fundamental cognition, insofar as
it has formed itself, further seeks to assert itself.

{\small\emph{Note~1.} When we cognize all things in their ultimate grounds, we must
also come to cognize our very cognizing---which belongs among these all things---in
its ultimate groundedness. In the total order of things in which we move, we find,
among other things, cognizing beings; the existence of these, with the whole
striving after cognition that appears in them, must then be grounded in this order
of things, and must, as regards its nature and mode of existence and as regards its
reality, rest upon it. ---This too will be seen when the subjective principal task
is pursued. Even the subjective-idealist solution of it---according to which it is
assumed that the existence presenting itself to us has no proper objectivity, but
has reality, validity, and existence only in and for our consciousness, or our
knowing of and with ourselves---nonetheless turns, in seeking to determine the
reality of our representing, on the question what we are to take the whole
objectivity given to us to be; and when it teaches us that we live only in a world
that presents itself to us---of whose reality, independently of this its
self-presentation to us, we can never speak, since we can never have to do with
anything other than what presents itself to us---it thereby nonetheless determines
both our existence and the mode of existence of what we call the objective, or call
the things over against us; and it does so in such a way that we nonetheless also
find ourselves over against them, and so must reckon ourselves too among the
totality of things; for ``thing'' here means in general only something that is
spoken of and that can be the object of an assuming and judging. But cognition we
nonetheless find again as something definite in the midst of that totality; and if
this totality is only a system of necessary representations, then our endeavor of
cognition finds itself nonetheless inserted as a member within this system of
necessary representations. But the subjective principal task's inclusion under the
objective is seen especially when we come to that solution of it which was set
forth above by way of remark (\S~3, Note~3), or even when we merely follow the path
taken there; and this principal task has therefore also, in another form, been able
and bound to arise in the historical course of philosophy's development in such a
way that the matter was entirely taken up under the pursuit of the objective
tasks---namely as the question of the relation between soul and body, and the
connected question of the relation between individuality and surrounding world;
whereby both question and solution have taken shape in such a way that the task
could and had to extend also to plants---indeed to everything that, as a member of
the world, stands under the determining influences of a surrounding world, and yet
acts out of its own peculiar nature.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~2.} Here the question arises how far a doctrine such as
Locke's---which aims only at elucidating the fundamental constitution and the
source or origin of all cognizing, as it is found to be under exact psychical
observation---can be reckoned, both in and for itself and with respect to its
effect, not to explicative philosophy alone (under which it straightforwardly
belongs), but also to philosophy proper, or speculative philosophy---irrespective,
moreover, of what worth and validity we may grant it, on which it here does not
depend. ---At bottom it aims only at elucidating what I have called the
\textit{universam cognoscendi rationem}; but it has come forward as---or at least
been made to hold as---that in which not only philosophy's whole subjective
principal task was to find its settlement and quieting, but also the foundation for
the question of the ultimate grounds and fundamental relations of all things was to
be so elucidated that in this respect one could refer to nothing but empirical
inquiry [Empirien]---so that speculative philosophy in the objective respect would
thereby be dismissed, and thus the objective principal task, as a speculative task,
decided. So far, then, such a philosophy must be regarded as one that has had to do
with both philosophy's subjective and its objective task. It has sought, in an
all-comprehending way, to set up a fundamental cognition concerning the ultimate
grounds of all cognizing, and concerning the way to the finding-out and
determination of the ultimate fundamental relations of all things.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~3.} Concerning a skepticism such as Hume's, something similar is
to be noted; but Greek skepticism especially---as that which sprang from the whole
course of the fundamental-philosophical movements and bears their stamp---must now
be acknowledged as philosophy in the proper sense, insofar, namely, as here, as
there, it is only a question of the propaedeutic concept of philosophy that holds
for the history of speculative philosophy, and insofar too as a totality of
philosophizing that neither leads nor is meant to lead to a philosophical science,
but only to the development of a philosophical way of viewing and of taking life,
can be called philosophy. ---But it is now also seen that the concept of philosophy
here given fits that skepticism entirely; for the skeptical doctrine is not that on
the empirical standpoint nothing is given that can be assumed with reliability, but
that, when one goes to the ultimate grounds, nothing can be set up as incontestably
certain---save that skepticism could not, of course, itself come forward as a
doctrine, since it had of course to admit that it too must become the object of its
own doubt, and so in the last instance dared not come forward as a proper
fundamental cognition, wherefore it was to hold only as an ultimate fundamental
quieting, aimed at bringing about the ἀταραξία of the mind.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~4.} When Kant, in order to work toward a similar quieting, sought
to establish that there can be question only of a merely subjective reality and
validity of all our cognizing, but that such a validity now also stands as firm as
can be needful for us---whereas all properly speculative striving would be an
attempt at a transcendence beyond all the capacity of our reason---he has thereby
not only treated the subjective principal task, but has also sought to settle the
objective, namely by cutting it off or dismissing it. ---Fichte, on the other hand,
in the period when he declared his Doctrine of Science [Videnskabslære] to be only
a carrying-through of Kantianism, and could declare it such, went nevertheless,
precisely by this carrying-through, so far beyond Kant that he at bottom allowed
himself that transcendence, in taking the matter speculatively; for he sought
speculatively to clarify and justify the Kantian doctrine by leading it back to a
deeper foundation.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~5.} It must here be noted generally that our determination of the
concept of philosophy leaves it undecided whether there are such ultimate grounds
of things as can be cognized by us in a rational way. Our conceptual determination
therefore holds also for those who, on religious grounds and with regard to
positive revelation, reject all philosophy that would venture into the region of
the divine; for they do so because they deny that human reason, at least in
humanity's fallen state, is capable of pressing through to the highest truths---%
which is the same as pressing through to the ultimate fundamental relations of
existence. ---It must moreover be noted generally that we have not set up
philosophy as a doctrine of the ultimate grounds of things, but only as a
fundamental cognition that forms itself under a striving to reach the ultimate
grounds---that is, a doctrine with respect to them, or concerning them.\par}

\begin{center}\large\S~11.\end{center}
\markboth{\S~11}{\S~11}
\begin{center}Philosophy steps up to what is otherwise given. ---A survey of the
sum-total of all this.\end{center}

As that which is to press through to the ultimate grounds and fundamental
relations of existence, or of all things, philosophy presupposes that existence,
with all it contains, has asserted itself in us, and has been apprehended and is
known, and now presents itself as that which philosophy steps up to. Philosophy
thus forms itself over against, and in a certain opposition to, both the whole
given existence, with what it contains, and the whole body of all that cognizing
through which everything that is now to find its ultimate clarification in
philosophy antecedently asserts itself in us. Everything that philosophy is thus
thought to step up to we call---without, however, meaning to deny that philosophy
too can form itself only by being given to us---the sum-total of the Given
[Indbegrebet af det Givne], which, moreover, is given us partly in an empirical,
partly in an a priori way, and in the latter case again partly in a sympathetic,
partly in an intelligent way.

A survey of all this Given will teach us that we have to distinguish therein the
following spheres:

1) The domain of outer experience or empirical inquiry [Empirie] (of natural
science and human history), in which cognitions form themselves along the way of
explicative pursuit, and the first foundation of cognition is observation by means
of the outer senses---whether under an immediate becoming-aware or experiencing,
or under the apprehension of what others have thus observed and let come to us
through so-called transmission (tradition); but the main thing becomes the
understanding's penetration into what is thus given, since properly empirical
science can consist only in that which is drawn from, or stands out from, the
immediately sensed or experienced and the transmitted, with abiding validity and
for abiding use.

2) The domain of independent a priori construction or operation, in which we
ourselves construct, constitute, produce the objects by an activity that is not
reproductive, but aims at realizing thoughts conceived in advance, which then form
themselves \textit{a priori}. It is then here this a priori element (the
understanding's own inner dictates) that becomes the object of study. But the a
priori element we have to do with here aims as yet only at constituting finite
objects and at reflecting on the relations that thereby come forward. ---In the
mathematical sciences, both pure mathematics and the mathematical-mechanical
sciences, we find the greatest and most telling example of such a domain; but
examples can also be found elsewhere, for instance in versification and in several
spheres of the poetic-aesthetic art, likewise in certain parts of such sciences as
do not otherwise belong here---of which syllogistics, grammatical syntax in
certain respects, and others, can furnish examples.

3) The domain of inner, psychical observation, which we shall come to call the
domain of explicative philosophy.

For the history of philosophy must bring us to distinguish two kinds of
philosophy, in that from a) the philosophy proper treated above---which we call
the speculative, but may also call the constitutive---we distinguish b) a great
body of sciences that have also been called philosophy, and go under that name,
although they are only explicative, or each pursue its own certain determinate
given in its givenness, without going back to the ultimate grounds of all things,
and thus belong under the domain of the explicative sciences, like those named
above under No.~1.

To this domain of inner psychical observation, or of explicative philosophy,
belong:

a) The domain of inner experience, psychology, in which the objects of our
observation and pursuit are our inner states of soul, with all that stirs in them,
insofar, namely, as it \textit{de facto} asserts itself (so that there is no
question of any ought, anything to be heeded as the all-valid---though certainly
the sense for the all-valid, and the human being's relation to it, as it is
\textit{de facto} given, must here be taken into account). That part of the
considerations belonging here which concerns the way in which all cognition forms
itself has especially been called ideology.

b) The domain of mere reflection-philosophy, in which the a priori element that
underlies our whole thinking and the pursuit, thereby called forth, of the
relations in the world---and so underlies empirical inquiry, as governing and
guiding the whole empirical apprehension and pursuit---is observed and explicated
in its universal validity or universality (thus also as the valid-for-us). Here,
then, in the factual, the universally valid that comes forward therein is studied.
To this belong explicative logic and ontology.

c) The domain of explicative philosophy of the Idea, in which the inner
manifestations of the infinite and eternal---insofar as they come forward as such,
and also show themselves as the higher on which it depends both what we ourselves
are to be ensouled and governed by, and by which the goods of life are rightly and
thoroughly to be assessed---become objects of explication and explicative
clarification. To this belong explicative philosophy of religion, moral
philosophy, and aesthetics.

But if we now look back to speculative philosophy, which is now to step up to all
these domains, we note that, since it too has after all asserted itself in the
world, and is thus to be found in the world, our speculative philosophizing must
also enter into relation to what is thereby given; and so we come to add still:

4) the domain of speculative philosophizing itself, insofar as it is given us in a
historical way---that is, the history of philosophy.

{\small\emph{Note~1.} It has its difficulty to distinguish sufficiently the whole
domain of mathematics from the rest of the a priori, so that an essential
difference may come to light between mathematics and explicative philosophy. As
for the difference of mathematics from speculative philosophy, that is easily seen.
Mathematics goes back only to its own ultimate grounds, its own ultimate
foundation, and is at every point able to lead everything back to it. But it does
not go back to, or seek to go back to, the ultimate grounds of all things, nor to
the question of the ultimate ground and groundedness of all cognizing. It has, in
general, nothing whatever to do with the tasks of existence itself; it moves only
in a realm of possibilities (something it has, moreover, in common with a merely
explicative formal logic), and is, of all the sciences, the one that least of all
comes into contact with the speculative. ---But why is it not reckoned under
explicative philosophy? If we consider especially---not geometry, which Kant, as
Schmidten notes in his work \textit{Om Mathematikens Væsen}, p.~30, alone seems to
have had in view where he would determine the difference between philosophy and
mathematics---but arithmetic and algebra, as the theory of functions, then we must
acknowledge that it may be asked why mathematics, insofar as it elucidates the
nature and essence of calculation, is not just as much to be called philosophical
as the explicative logic that elucidates the nature and essence of thinking;
especially since calculation, subjectively, both underlies and can come to
underlie every exact apprehension of the Given and penetrating cognition therein;
and is also, objectively, to be regarded as underlying the whole of existence
itself, just as the logical has such an objective reality and existence as
foundation for existence; so that, whether explicative logic be counted as
philosophy because what it sets out belongs to the general fundamental, or because
it explicates the inner a priori in us, there would seem to be ground for
reckoning mathematics too as belonging thereto.\par}

{\small Here, then, the following is to be noted. It is given us that mathematics
is not reckoned as philosophy, whereas simple explicative logic and ontology,
provided they are taken in the right, thorough way, are reckoned as such. This
usage, and the view expressed through it, is what we are to clarify by finding out
what it is that has made possible its formation. Thus: we are to seek out the
difference that may here show itself. And then we might first note that logic and
ontology always aim at the more fundamental, the ultimate fundamental; for what
they explicate underlies mathematics as well as every other science. It is the
direct coming-forward of an ultimate underlying that is here explicated. This
difference, however, could not be enough, since psychology is reckoned wholly to
explicative philosophy, although it does not aim wholly at anything generally
fundamental. What must become the main point is that the apprehension and
observation, and the pursuit of the observed, that take place in the cited domains
of explicative philosophy, are to be called psychical, in a sense in which that
which occurs in mathematics cannot be so called. For although in mathematics too
there is observed what the a priori in us has to tell us with respect to the
relations that occur and are to be pursued, and the tasks that are to be solved,
yet these relations themselves---indeed everything that is observed, that is, the
objects of the consideration---cannot be said to be of psychical nature (for the
possible application of mathematics in psychology is not here in question). In
logic and ontology, by contrast, there are observed partly
\emph{consciousness's own facts}, partly the \emph{a priori that constitutes the
life of consciousness itself}; whereas, when in mathematics the formula of the
parabola is found out, or any function whatever is determined and pursued, this may
indeed be said to be something we learn from the a priori in us alone, but not
something in which this a priori is itself observed, nor something that constitutes
our own existence of soul. If, on the other hand, someone were to demonstrate the
mathematical as a foundation in our reflection on many relations in the world, and
even in our life of soul, then this would indeed be explicative philosophy, but
would then belong not to mathematics but to psychology.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~2.} When the all-valid is to be studied in and through the
factual, the apprehension of the factual becomes of essential importance in and
for the doctrine of the all-valid. This holds especially with respect to
explicative philosophy of the Idea, where therefore---although it must throughout
come about through one's own observation, so that others' observations are only to
be taken as those that are to serve this end---the history of the sciences becomes
of a far different and greater importance for the sciences themselves than that
which, for example, the history of mathematics can have for mathematicians. But to
go so far as to make the whole philosophy of religion consist in the doctrine of
religion's historical course of development, as has happened in our time, is not to
take the matter rightly.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~3.} It has, furthermore, its difficulty to distinguish
sufficiently between reflection-philosophy and explicative philosophy of the Idea,
since in both the all-valid comes forward as such, so that an ought asserts itself
just as much in logic as in morals---wherefore Treschow too reckoned logic to
practical philosophy (which, in his work \textit{Om Philos.\ Natur og Dele}, he
divided into logic, moral philosophy, aesthetics, and teleology, according to the
ends: the true, the good, the beautiful, and the useful). But when one notes that
the admonition to respect the logical demands for truth's sake---that is, out of
love of truth---is of moral nature, one will see that the ought that arises in
logic is only a ``so it ought to be,'' not a ``so \emph{we} ought;'' and that
former ought is therefore rather a must. ---The proper ought expresses a mastery
as such, or aims at what is to be ruling in the one governed, when this one shows
itself as such---indeed as desiring and willing.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~4.} That sciences of mixed nature arise, we readily become aware.
We have examples of this in physics and astronomy, insofar as in both the
empirical and the mathematical interlock. In psychology, alongside what belongs to
the domain of inner experience, that which belongs to the rest of explicative
philosophy of the Idea will also come to speech. In aesthetics, under the
Idea-philosophical observation and development, one must constantly look to the
fundamental aesthetic concepts that have historically asserted themselves. Moral
philosophy and philosophy of religion cannot be carried through without
empirically given conditions---to which indeed the whole of sinfulness
belongs---coming into consideration. ---But everywhere the thorough consideration
and orientation requires that one be able to become aware of what belongs to each
domain.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~5.} We saw here that psychology takes on a mixed nature by
entering upon the domain of the Idea-philosophical. But in yet another respect it
shows itself as mixed, since outer observation---namely of others' life and
conduct, that is, something belonging to the domain of outer empirical
inquiry---must here be taken into account, together with what the inner psychical
observation, which becomes the foundation, teaches us. ---The philosophy of
language, too, must develop itself through a union of empirical study (of the
historically given languages) and psychical observation.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~6.} The philosophy of history is still to be named here as mixed,
since in it historical apprehension and pursuit must go in one with an observation
that must be of psychical nature. For the philosophy of history is to clarify the
whole life-course of humanity with respect to what is to be noted therein in the
respect of spirituality, and the development it has taken in that respect. The
fundamental moments of the life of humanity, and the course of their movements, are
then to be observed; but thus the consideration becomes psycho-biological.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~7.} We must still direct a special attention to certain sciences
that form genuine intermediate links, in that they present genuine synthetic
unities of something given in an outer, historical way, and something belonging to
the domain of explicative philosophy of the Idea. ---What is meant hereby is a)
so-called positive revelation and positive theology, and b) positive jurisprudence
and politics. In the domain of the first, something that rests essentially on
historical transmission is to be brought to live and rule in us, and to ensoul and
fill us, in the same way as that in which what belongs to the domain of philosophy
of the Idea makes itself both Idea-moving and practically effective in us; and the
former must therefore be expounded in a corresponding way. In politics, as well as
in the domain of the positive jurisprudence intrinsically belonging to it, there
constantly meet---at least as regards so-called administration and the endeavors
belonging to the improvement of legislation---the regard for what natural right and
the care of the state demand, with the regard for what, in a given state, has
partly been positively laid down and thereby set going, partly historically
developed; and both what the one and what the other demands in its consequence must
be heeded, and a solution of the tasks sought that can at once satisfy in both
respects---that is, both in the Idea-philosophical and in the empirical-historical
respect.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~8.} That, finally, explicative and speculative consideration too
can be united in one scientific development and presentation must still be
noted---of which the present philosophical propaedeutic of ours may be an
example.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~9.} Of a different nature than the sciences of mixed nature
discussed so far are the more general sciences, in which that which shows itself as
generally underlying in several different domains is separated out and considered
by itself in its inner fundamental nature, universally valid for these
domains.\par}

{\small Such a science is general biology, insofar as in it the life in plants and
animals, the inner life of soul in the human being as individual, and the life in
humanity are had in view at once and equally.\par}

\begin{center}\large\S~12.\end{center}
\markboth{\S~12}{\S~12}
\begin{center}Exposition of the given concept of philosophy: three chief points of
view herein.\end{center}

To the sum-total of all that we have called the Given, and have now set out before
us in a survey, philosophy steps up---by which, now as hitherto, we constantly mean
only the speculative, not the explicative---in order to press through to its
ultimate grounds and fundamental relations, and, in force of the cognition of
these, to clarify it from the ground up. But since it is thus called forth by the
all-comprehending spirit of inquiry that comes into motion in the scrutinizing and
grounding subjectivity---which, in order to attain a philosophy, must first
philosophize its way to it---philosophy, with respect to the fundamental relation
between the true and the subjectivity in which the true is to assert itself,
presents itself to us under three chief points of view, namely: I.~with respect to
the cognizing subjectivity and philosophy's significance for it; II.~with respect
to the coming-together into unity of the subjective and the objective; III.~purely
in and for itself, without regard to what stirs in the cognizing subjectivity.

\begin{center}\large\S~13, a.\end{center}
\markboth{\S~13a}{\S~13a}
\begin{center}Exposition of the concept of philosophy in the first chief respect.\end{center}

With respect to I.~the subjectivity that cognizes and strives after thorough
cognition, the spirit of inquiry in motion lets us see philosophy first of all as
a) the fundamental cognition that is to proceed from a calling-to-account that goes
to the ground, through which everything, each thing severally, is to establish
itself for us in the validity it is to have for us, by, as it were, exhibiting its
title to assert itself. ---But hereby b) everything, in its whole compass, shows
itself as that which presents a problem to be solved; and philosophy has therefore
been able to be represented as that which is to solve the riddle of existence,
after itself first having let such a riddle arise and take shape for us. ---But
since to this attaches the remark that the philosophical questions often take the
turn of asking after the possibility of that whose actuality no one doubts---indeed
whose actuality is perhaps formally established in advance---we see philosophy as
that which may be said c) to aim at elucidating the possibility of the actual,
which then means explicability, intelligibility, comprehensibility, where actuality
and thinking are seen to meet as those that seek a coming-together into unity and a
so-called reconciliation---which, however, becomes at the same time a leveling and
uniting of the many mutually opposing moments of existence itself, even for the
cognizing consideration. ---But since, under all such thinking, the spirit of
investigation comes into motion as a spirit of discussion or debate, d) philosophy
comes forward as that which forms itself in an all-comprehending way in force of
overcome dubitation---that is, according to an affirmation in force of overcome
negation. (Cf.\ \textit{Om Erkj.\ og Gr.}, \S~20, p.~162.)

But thus philosophy will present itself to us, on the one side, as that which is to
bring the subjectivity to e) a full coming-to-clarity with itself
(Selbstverständigung, as Jacobi called it), and on the other side as that which is
to lead to f) a full coming-to-clarity with the totality in its totality, and
thereby to full orientedness within it; from which g) an all-comprehending
satisfaction in the domain of cognition must follow.

{\small\emph{Note~1.} Kant began the whole work of his philosophy (at the beginning
of his \textit{Kritik der reinen Vernunft}) with the question: how are synthetic
propositions \textit{a priori} possible?---after he had first established the actual
existence and reality of such propositions. Later this led to the question: how is
a valid cognition possible at all? But this question, with the one lying within
it---how is an objectively valid sensation possible?---had already been in full
motion in the Cartesian school, in Malebranche and Leibniz, just as it had already
fully come forward in antiquity. ---How is speculative philosophy possible? we shall
come to ask further on. ---How is human freedom possible? how is evil possible? are
questions that naturally take precisely this form.\par}

{\small\emph{Note~2.} The nominal concept of philosophy that Fichte gives in the
philosophical journal published by him together with Niethammer (year 1797, number
1), as well as the one he set forth in his latest lectures in Berlin, may still be
brought to remembrance here. In the former he sets up philosophy as that which is to
render the whole of experience explicable by deducing it \textit{a priori}, where
then, once philosophy had completed its work, one would have to see whether what it
had constructed \textit{a priori} corresponded to experience in such a way that the
task could be called solved. Since we possess the whole of experience only in our
knowing, he did not thereby deviate from his earlier concept of philosophy,
according to which he had given philosophy the name of \textit{Wissenschaftslehre},
as that which was to make the whole of human knowing in its wholeness---as knowing
and the sum-total of knowing in general---the object of explanation. In his latest
lectures he stated that every science had its phenomenon---he used precisely this
expression---to explain. The phenomenon that philosophy had to explain was now
human knowing itself in general, or as a whole, als ein Wißthum. This phenomenon
had now first to be expounded, then explained. The former he did in the doctrine
\textit{von den Thatsachen des Bewußtseyns}, which came in the first part of his
course; ---the latter he did in the \textit{Wissenschaftslehre} proper. One easily
sees that, in similarity to the way in which Kant began his \textit{Critique}, he
could have begun with the question: How is knowing possible at all?\par}

{\small\emph{Note~3.} We here come to recall the often-mentioned but, as it seems,
in our time commonly misunderstood or unknown definition of philosophy that Wolff
gives in his \textit{Discursus præliminaris de philosophia in genere} (prefixed to
his \textit{Logik}). It runs, in \S~29, as follows: \textit{Philosophia est scientia
possibilium, qvatenus esse possunt.} Wolff says of it in the note that he first
found it in the year 1703, that in the year 1705 he communicated it to the Breslau
church and school inspector Caspar Neumann, against whose objections he defended it
in a private letter, but that he first made it public in the preface to his
\textit{Elementa Aërometriæ}. This, however, does not give the information I had
expected to find in it. The meaning of that definition, on the other hand, is
sufficiently elucidated in the cited \textit{Discursus} itself---not directly,
indeed, but in several ways indirectly. The words on which it especially depends are
the last: \textit{qvatenus esse possunt}. In a preceding paragraph (\S~6)
\textit{cognitio philosophica} is defined as being \textit{cognitio rationis eorum,
qvæ sunt vel fiunt}; and one sees that he regards all rational cognition of
that---or all insight into that---which is or happens as philosophical, whereas the
mere knowledge of the factual is set up as \textit{cognitio historica}; and the
third of the three kinds of cognition he distinguishes, the mathematical, is to aim
at the exact determination of finite things by determining their quantitative
relations. When he now, in treating more closely this difference between historical,
philosophical, and mathematical cognition, says among other things in \S~17:
\textit{Aliud est nosse factum, aliud perspicere rationem facti, aliud denique
determinare qvantitatem rerum}; when in \S~7 it reads: \textit{Differt cognitio
philosophica ab historica, haec enim in nuda facti notitia subsistit, illa vero
ulterius progressa rationem facti palam facit, ut intelligatur, cur istiusmodi qvid
fieri possit}; and when one now, in the examples he adduces, meets all sorts of
phenomena that philosophy is to explain, one sees that his meaning must be that
philosophy is to render actuality explicable, by showing how everything in it is
able to be. ---Later, after that definition of \textit{cognitio philosophica} has
been followed by the above-cited definition of philosophy itself, it reads in \S~31:
\textit{In philosophia reddende est ratio, cur possibilia actum conseqvi possint},
and immediately after: \textit{In philosophia itaqve reddenda est ratio, qvomodo ea,
qvæ fieri possunt, actu fiant}; furthermore it reads in \S~32: \textit{qvodsi ergo
plura fuerint, qvorum unum perinde possibile est ac cetera, philosophia docere
debet, cur illud potius fiat aut fieri debeat, qvam cetera}; on which occasion
philosophy is spoken of as \textit{rationes eorum, qvæ sunt vel fiunt, reddens}, and
as that which therefore \textit{docere debet, qvænam sit ratio, ut in dato aliqvo
casu unum potius fiat, qvam alterum}. In \S~37 philosophy is named as that
\textit{qvæ rationem exponit, cur ea, qvæ sunt et fiunt, esse ac fieri possint}. In
\S~46, finally, we find the following definition of a philosopher: \textit{Philosophus
est, qvi rationem reddere potest eorum, qvæ sunt, vel esse possunt}; and on this
occasion philosophy is named immediately after as that \textit{cujus est rationem
reddere, cur possibilia actum conseqvi qveant, et unum potius fiat in dato casu qvam
alterum, qvod perinde possibile erat}. ---If one now compares all this, and a good
deal more, with that definition---\textit{Philosophia est scientia possibilium,
qvatenus esse possunt}---one sees that the weight must fall on the last words, and
we see that their meaning must be to indicate what has already been mentioned: that
philosophy is to elucidate how everything that \textit{de facto} is found to be, or
to happen, in nature (to which, according to \S~21, \textit{natura rationalis}, that
is, the life of consciousness, also belongs) is able to be---or what its real ground
of possibility is. With the actual facts and factually existing things philosophy
has nothing to do, insofar as it is a question of which they are; this belongs to
historical knowledge; but to elucidate the grounds of them---that is, to show what
calls forth or brings about their realization---is philosophy's task. But that which
brings about realization is not actuality itself, but is the real ground of
possibility of it. Thus we must without doubt interpret that definition.
---Philosophy moves in the domain of the thinkable---there is thinking back and
forth---so far we have to do with \textit{possibilia}; but now it is a matter of
determining what, out of all this thinkability, rises up as that which has reality
and constitutes the underlying element in actuality; which is then not the concrete
actual itself, but is that in which the actual has its ground, and from which,
accordingly, it is to be discerned how this actual is able to be (\textit{actum
conseqvi possit}). It is thus the realm of the thinkable that meets with that of the
actual; the former is to elucidate the latter, the latter is led back to the former
and taken up into it. Actuality is here without doubt thought as that which has an
inexhaustible realm of possibilities for its foundation---a representation one often
meets in the eighteenth century.\par}
